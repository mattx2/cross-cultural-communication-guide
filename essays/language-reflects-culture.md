# How Language Reflects Culture: What Grammar Reveals About Values

Every language is a fossil record of its culture's priorities. The features a language chooses to encode — and the ones it ignores — tell you what that culture considers important enough to build into every sentence its speakers will ever produce.

## Formality and Social Hierarchy

### Korean: Seven Levels of Politeness

Korean has seven distinct speech levels, each signaling a different degree of respect and social distance. The verb ending changes depending on whether you're talking to:

- A close friend your age (*banmal* — casual speech)
- An older colleague (*jondaenmal* — polite formal)
- A customer or elder (*hapsioche* — highest formal)

Getting the level wrong isn't just a grammar mistake — it's a social statement. Using casual speech with someone who expects formal speech is insulting. Using overly formal speech with a close friend creates uncomfortable distance.

This grammatical system reflects a society where understanding your relationship to others — their age, status, and your degree of closeness — is so important that it's encoded in every verb of every sentence.

### Japanese: Three Layers of Politeness

Japanese goes even further with *keigo* (敬語), which includes three distinct systems:

- **Sonkeigo** (尊敬語): Honorific language that elevates the listener
- **Kenjougo** (謙譲語): Humble language that lowers the speaker
- **Teineigo** (丁寧語): General polite language

A single action like "eating" has different verbs depending on who's doing it and who you're talking to. "I eat" uses humble language (lowering yourself), while "you eat" uses honorific language (elevating the other person). The grammar itself performs social work.

### Contrast: English

English has "you" — one word for everyone from your best friend to a head of state. Old English had *thou* (informal) and *ye/you* (formal), but the distinction collapsed centuries ago. This reflects a culture that, over time, moved toward treating social equals as the default.

## What Languages Force You to Express

### Evidentiality in Turkish

Turkish grammar requires speakers to mark whether they personally witnessed what they're describing or whether they heard it secondhand. The sentence "It rained" has two forms:

- *Yağmur yağdı* — I saw it rain (direct evidence)
- *Yağmur yağmış* — I heard it rained (reported/inferred)

English can express this distinction, but it doesn't *require* it. Turkish does. Every statement about the past forces the speaker to be transparent about their source of information.

### Gendered Nouns in Spanish and German

Spanish and German assign grammatical gender to all nouns — and research suggests this subtly affects how speakers think about objects. In a famous study by Lera Boroditsky, German speakers described a bridge (*die Brücke*, feminine) using words like "beautiful," "elegant," and "slender," while Spanish speakers described a bridge (*el puente*, masculine) as "strong," "sturdy," and "towering."

The objects are identical. The grammar colored the perception.

### Time in Mandarin Chinese

Mandarin has no verb tenses. Instead of changing the verb form (walk/walked/will walk), speakers use context words and time markers. "I go yesterday," "I go tomorrow," and "I go now" all use the same verb form.

This doesn't mean Mandarin speakers can't think about time — they clearly can. It means their language doesn't force them to categorize every action as past, present, or future the way English does. Some researchers (following the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis) argue this creates subtle differences in how time is mentally represented.

## Color and Perception

Russian has separate basic words for light blue (*goluboy*) and dark blue (*siniy*), while English just has "blue." Research at MIT found that Russian speakers can distinguish between shades of blue faster than English speakers — the linguistic distinction creates a perceptual advantage.

The Pirahã people of the Amazon have no precise number words — they use approximate terms like "few" and "many." Research by Peter Gordon found that they struggled with exact quantity tasks that preschoolers in numerate cultures handle easily.

Language doesn't imprison thought, but it does create well-worn grooves that make certain distinctions easier or harder to notice.

## Kinship Terms

English has a relatively simple kinship system: "uncle" covers your mother's brother, your father's brother, and their spouses. Chinese has distinct terms for each:

- 伯父 (*bófu*) — father's older brother
- 叔父 (*shūfu*) — father's younger brother
- 舅舅 (*jiùjiu*) — mother's brother
- 姑父 (*gūfu*) — father's sister's husband
- 姨夫 (*yífu*) — mother's sister's husband

This reflects a culture where family structure, birth order, and which side of the family someone belongs to are important enough to encode in everyday vocabulary. An English speaker groups these relationships together; a Chinese speaker distinguishes each one automatically.
